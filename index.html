What is this?

An autonomous monitoring agent for humanitarian assistance and disaster response (HADR). Every morning it reads three public sources — USGS (earthquake detection, minutes after the event), GDACS (the EU/UN impact model covering cyclones, floods, quakes, volcanoes, wildfires and droughts) and ReliefWeb (UN OCHA's curated disaster records) — merges their views of the same disasters into single incidents, and publishes a one-page situation report. When nothing significant changed overnight, it publishes nothing: the report you see is only ever news.

Why does it work this way?

Because the naive version of this tool is worse than useless. Watching disaster feeds sounds simple until you try it:

  • The feeds are a firehose of noise. USGS alone lists hundreds of earthquakes a day, almost all harmless. So this monitor never triages on magnitude — it uses the humanitarian-impact signals the sources themselves publish (USGS PAGER, GDACS alert colours: expected human consequence, not physical size). A magnitude-7.5 in the deep ocean stays green; a shallow 6.0 under an unprepared city goes red.
  • Disaster data is rewritten constantly. Magnitudes get revised, alert levels upgraded days later, entire events deleted as false triggers. This monitor keeps a ledger of what it has already told you, so an upgrade becomes an escalation headline, a deletion becomes a printed retraction — never a silent rewrite of history.
  • Silence must mean something. A quiet dashboard is only reassuring if a broken pipeline would look different from a calm world. Every run commits proof-of-life state, failed feeds get named in a staleness banner on the report, and the pipeline tests itself before every run.

Where it's headed: richer ReliefWeb data once UN OCHA approves API access (the monitor reads its public RSS feed today), a Telegram channel that answers questions and pushes changed-morning headlines, and possibly official tsunami warnings (tsunami.gov) as a fourth source. The direction is always the same: fewer things to check, more reasons to trust the silence.

Who is it for, and what decision does it speed up?

Anyone who starts the day with the question “did anything happen overnight that needs my attention?” — a duty officer, a programme manager with teams in the field, a researcher tracking humanitarian crises, or anyone who would otherwise open three websites and try to reconcile them. The decision it speeds up is morning triage: in under two minutes you know what's new, what escalated, what was corrected, and what can safely be ignored — with a link to the primary source for every claim, so acting on it starts from evidence, not from a summary.

What it deliberately does not do

  • No conflict or epidemic monitoring. Its sources cover natural hazards. A war will not appear here — pretending otherwise would be false coverage, which is worse than a stated gap.
  • No real-time paging. It is a daily briefing, not an alarm. If minutes matter for your mission, you need a warning system, and this page will not pretend to be one.
  • No tsunami warnings. The earthquake feed's “tsunami” flag does not mean a tsunami exists. Real warnings come from tsunami.gov — so this monitor never uses the word unless a source that can issue one does.
  • No original judgments. Alert levels are USGS/GDACS impact estimates, reported as such. The report's prose is generated, but every statement must trace to a machine-recorded fact — the writing step is forbidden from adding knowledge.
  • No substitute for authorities. It links to sources and never overrides national warning systems or official guidance.
  • No completeness below its floor. Small events outside the feeds' reliable coverage (roughly magnitude 4.5+ internationally) simply are not in the data, and the monitor does not guess about them.

How it earns trust

A deterministic pipeline fetches, reconciles and triages; a language model is allowed to do exactly one thing — write the report's prose from a machine-produced facts file — and is never allowed to fetch data or decide whether anything happened. State is committed to a public git repository, so the monitor's entire memory and every report it has ever published are auditable, commit by commit. The design document spells out every rule above, including the ones that were hard to get right.
